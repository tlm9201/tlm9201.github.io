Entry One: The Merchant


Note: All entries are wrote as if they were from the point of view of the protagonist "Santiago."

It was quite a long night. I had just finished sleeping at some old abandoned Church, on my journey to sell my flock's wool to a merchant. I had met the merchant's daughter, who she talked to me a year prior. We talked and talked, and she questioned me why I am a shepherd, amazed at how I could read. I could not answer her, so I discussed how being a shepherd allows you to journey and my fascinating adventures. I felt an odd sensation throughout. Throughout my years of being a shepherd, I always wanted to travel and never permanently stay somewhere, yet now, I could only think of staying with her. Her enticing eyes and black hair desired me. The merchant had then finally appeared and asked for four sheep's wool, telling me to return next year. I was disappointed that I had to leave, so I said goodbye and moved on.

My father always wanted me to be a priest, but I insisted that I become a traveler and see farther lands. He told me that travelers only crave their past travels and do not change or mature. Although he didn't like the idea of me becoming a shepherd, he still encouraged me to travel and follow my dreams. I sighed at this thought, knowing that my father would do the same and travel, but couldn't because he would not afford it. I recalled my prior dream at the Church and decided to visit a woman who could interpret it.

Entry Two: My Dream

Today, I had visited the interpreter. She was odd and mysterious, holding my hands, she says a Gypsy prayer. I tremble as I recalled my fear of being taken by a Gypsy. I yanked my hands back and insisted that I didn’t need a palm reading. Then, I explain my dream: I was in a field with my flock, with a child wandering around them. The child takes me to the pyramids in Egypt, where he tells him about a treasure amongst them. The child then explains where the treasure is, but my dream cut out. I hadn't thought much of this, as I thought it was absurd to think there was a treasure I could obtain in the pyramids.

The interpreter falls silent for a minute, after returning she responds: telling me that I did not have to pay for her services, but in return, she wanted ten percent of the treasure. I chuckled at the thought of this, but I was comforted that I didn't have to pay. After my visit to the interpreter, I bought a new book in the village and sat down to read it.

An old man approached me, calling himself Melchizedek and the "King of Salem." I had never been to Salem and thought he was lying. He explains that he would help me find the treasure if I gave him one-tenth of my sheep. I was skeptical and assumed that he worked with the interpreter. To prove that he didn't, he told me my mother and father's name.

Melchizedek told me about a force called a Personal Legend, similar to a dream in which you want to accomplish ever since you are young. But as time goes on, people feel that they can't accomplish their Personal Legend and end up forgetting it. I ended up giving him one-tenth of my sheep, in return, he gave me two stones that would guide me to omens.

Entry Three: The Crystal Merchant

After I foolishly got robbed, I seeked work to receive some change. The merchants began to set up their shops around me in the marketplace. Some of the merchants speak Arabic and some speak Spanish, yet they still understand each other without words. As I wander around, I noticed a lonely crystal merchant. I offered him that I could clean the glasses in compensation for food. The merchant explained that he would not let me clean because the Koran tells him to feed the hungry anyways. He then took me to a nearby cafe and ordered me food. The crystal merchant then told me that he would offer me a job because it was a good omen when people came in while I was cleaning the glasses. I was relieved and took the job.

As I continued with my job, I got more and more annoyed at the crystal merchant. He was mean and strict, and the work was very repetitive--although, I was making good money, but not quick enough. I offered him to build a display case to show off the glasses and attract more customers. He was not very keen of this idea and feared the glasses would break. I also proposed that he sold tea in crystal glasses for customers in the hot summer months. The merchant--although skeptical at first--accepted my ideas and customers began to rapidly increase. As the customers increased, he also had to higher more employees. I soon realize that I have plenty of money and enough to buy two times my original flock. So, I told the merchant that I would return to Tarifa to buy a flock. The crystal merchant said farewells and we continued on our different paths.

Entry Four: The Caravan

Through the next coming days, I prepared myself for the caravan that lied ahead of me. The caravan I was taking was through the desert to Egypt for the search of the treasure. I meet an Englishman, who is studying alchemy and is in search of a specific alchemist. In return, I told my story of the crystal merchant and such. I felt a strange bond with him; he reads as I do, and is in search of his Personal Legend. Throughout the caravan, we decide to switch occupations. I would try to constantly read and he would look through the desert as I did. As the nights passed on, I befriended others and continued to share my story with the alchemist, he compared me and my successes to the “Soul of the World.”

When we finally arrived to the desert oasis of “Al-Fayoum,” I was amazed at the sheer size and beauty of it. As the night progressed, I slept in a tent with others of my age where I shared my stories of being a shepard. The Englishman asked me to help find the alchemist, so I agreed and we searched for the rest of the day, with very little luck. I asked multiple people yet only came with success from the last one, who I fell in love with. Her name was Fatima. She said that desert spirits talk with the alchemist, and he is located in Al-Fayoum. The next day I waited for Fatima again, still love-struck and I tell her that love her. We continued to meet and talk each day.

Entry Five: The Treasure

As the days progress and turn into months, my endless journey continued. I had visions about the Soul of the World. I met chieftains, and gambled my life with my vision. I met a mysterious horseman, who asked me why I defied Allah’s will and tested my will. The horseman turned out to be the alchemist, who invited me inside of his tent and would guide me to my treasure. He ordered me to buy a horse by selling my camel. He took me to the pyramids, but I felt uneager to leave Fatima behind, but the alchemist insisted anyways. He explained that the Soul of the World must be found my immersing yourself in the desert, and pursing your heart’s dreams. We pass through dangerous war-zones and cross mountain ranges. The alchemist was very vague and not specific, moreover, he never explicitly told me what to do or how to do things, but instead let me learn it through my actions.

The alchemist explained that every Personal Legend has a fear, and that you should not let it stop you from pursuing it. My fear specifically was losing everything. The alchemist cleverly avoided death by appearing crazy and revealing we had valuables. I spoke with the wind, which I explain that I wished to reach everywhere in the world, like the wind. I speak to the sun, and convince that it is wrong. The tribesmen around me stood shocked, trying to take in what I had done. When I finally reached the pyramids, I dug for the gold but was ambushed by theives who ordered me to find it for them. I dug but could not find anything, so they beat me. I realized that the treasure wasn’t in the pyramids, but in my home under the sycamore tree all along. I smiled and felt contempt, and I had finally pursued my Personal Legend.
